Rewards and Costs in Online Decisions

CUREC Approval Reference: R71008/RE001

General Information

The aim of this study is to investigate how people incorporate rewards and costs into their decision-making. Motivational problems (e.g. apathy) are found in a range of psychiatric and neurological disorders, and affect how willing people are to put effort into tasks, and how they value rewards and costs.

We appreciate your interest in participating in this online study. You have been invited to participate as you are aged 18-80 years. Please read through this information before agreeing to participate by ticking the ‘yes’ box below.

You may ask any questions before deciding to take part by contacting the researcher (details below).

We (Sanjay Manohar & John Grogan) at the University of Oxford are investigating motivation. You will be shown objects on the screen and will either have to make decisions about them, or remember them for short durations. You will also be asked to answer some questions about yourself. The session will take maximum 2 hours to complete. No background knowledge is required. This data will be analysed for research purposes, and published in research journals.

Do I have to take part?

No, your participation is voluntary. If you do decide to take part, you may withdraw at any point during the study for any reason before submitting your answers by pressing the ‘Exit’ button/closing the browser.

What will happen to me if I take part in the research?

If you are happy to take part in the study, you will firstly be asked to consent online. Once this is done, you are given permission to commence the study part of the session. You will be asked to give some information about yourself, such as your age, gender, and level of education, and answer standardised questionnaires assessing mood, behaviour, general health and wellbeing. This is then followed by a number of computer-based tasks aimed at examining how rewards and costs affect your motivation during decision-making and memory.

Tasks involve abstract coloured objects which will be presented on the screen. Depending on the task, you will be instructed to either memorise or decide something about the stimuli. Prior to each task, you will complete a set of practice trials, allowing you to be familiarised with task procedures. You may withdraw from the study at any time, without giving a reason, by closing the study browser.

Are there any potential risks in taking part?

There is no known risk involved in the participation of this study.

Are there any benefits in taking part?

There will be no direct benefit to you from taking part in this research.

Expenses and payments

You will receive £8 per hour for participation, plus a bonus of up to £3 dependent on your performance in the task.

How will my data be used?

We will not be collecting data that can directly identify you (such as your name or date of birth), and we will take all reasonable measures to ensure that the information you provide remains confidential. The research data will be stored confidentially by assigning a code (study ID).

Your data will be stored in a password-protected file and may be used in academic publications. Your IP address will not be stored. We have included a ‘Prefer not to say’ option for each set of questions, should you prefer not to answer a particular question. Research data will be stored for as long as it is of continuing value to the research community.

Who will have access to my data?

We use www.qualtrics.com for online questionnaires. Qualtrics LLC is the data controller with respect to your personal data and, as such, will determine how your personal data is used.  Please see their privacy notice here https://www.qualtrics.com/privacy-statement. We use Pavlovia.org (https://pavlovia.org/docs/home/ethics), Gorilla.sc (https://gorilla.sc/privacy), or our own server on Amazon Mechanical Turk (https://www.mturk.com/privacy-notice) to run the online experiments, which are the data controllers with respect to your personal data. These sites will share only de-identified data with the University of Oxford, for the purposes of research.

The University of Oxford is the data controller with respect to your de-identified data collected in this study. The University will process your data for the purpose of the research outlined above. Research is a task that we perform in the public interest. Further information about your rights with respect to your data is available from https://compliance.admin.ox.ac.uk/individual-rights.
